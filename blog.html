Algebraic Geometry

Hilbert's Nullstellensatz

A fundamental theorem establishing a geometric correspondence between ideals and algebraic sets.

Weak Nullstellensatz

Theorem: Let $k$ be algebraically closed and $A = k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$. For an ideal $J \subseteq A$, the zero set $Z(J)$ is empty if and only if $J = A$.

Proof:

Assume $Z(J)=\varnothing$ and suppose $J \neq A$. Then $J$ is contained in a maximal ideal $\mathfrak m$. Since $J \subseteq \mathfrak m$, one has $Z(\mathfrak m) \subseteq Z(J)$; hence it suffices to show $Z(\mathfrak m)\neq\varnothing$.

The quotient $A/\mathfrak m$ is a field finitely generated as a $k$-algebra, so by Zariski’s Lemma it is a finite algebraic extension of $k$. As $k$ is algebraically closed, we obtain an isomorphism $A/\mathfrak m \cong k$.

Let $\varphi : A \to k$ denote the quotient map, and set $a_i = \varphi(x_i)$. Then the point $P = (a_1,\dots,a_n)$ satisfies $f(P)=0$ for all $f \in \mathfrak m$, so $P \in Z(\mathfrak m)$—a contradiction. Therefore $J=A$.

Strong Nullstellensatz

Theorem: For any ideal $J \subseteq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ one has

$$ I(Z(J)) = \sqrt{J}. $$

Easy Direction: $\sqrt{J} \subseteq I(Z(J))$

If $g \in \sqrt{J}$ then $g^e \in J$ for some $e$. For any $P \in Z(J)$, $g^e(P)=0$ implies $g(P)=0$, so $g$ vanishes on $Z(J)$.

Hard Direction: $I(Z(J)) \subseteq \sqrt{J}$

(Rabinowitsch Trick)

Let $g \in I(Z(J))$. Introduce a new variable $y$ and consider $A' = A[y]$ with the ideal

$$ J' = JA' + (1 - gy). $$

A point $(P,b)$ lies in $Z(J')$ only if $P \in Z(J)$ and $1 - g(P)b = 0$. But $g(P)=0$ on $Z(J)$, forcing $1=0$—a contradiction. Thus $Z(J')=\varnothing$.

By the Weak Nullstellensatz, $1 \in J'$, so we can write

$$ 1 = \sum_{i=1}^m a_i(x,y) f_i(x) + a_0(x,y)(1 - g(x)y) $$

in $A'$, where $J = (f_1,\dots,f_m)$.

Substitute $y = 1/g$ in the field of fractions to obtain

$$ 1 = \sum_{i=1}^m a_i(x,1/g)\, f_i(x). $$

Multiplying by a sufficiently large power $g^e$ clears denominators and yields

$$ g^e = \sum_{i=1}^m b_i(x) f_i(x) \in J. $$

Hence $g \in \sqrt{J}$, completing the proof.

Algebra

Universal Property of Free Groups

Allows the extension of any map from a set to a group into a unique group homomorphism.

Theorem: Let $S$ be a set and $F(S)$ the free group on $S$ with the canonical map $\iota: S \to F(S)$. For any group $G$ and any set map $\varphi: S \to G$, there exists a unique group homomorphism $\Phi: F(S) \to G$ such that $\Phi \circ \iota = \varphi$.

Proof: The elements of $F(S)$ consist of reduced words in the alphabet $S \cup S^{-1}$. Define $\Phi$ on a reduced word $w = s_1^{\epsilon_1} s_2^{\epsilon_2} \cdots s_n^{\epsilon_n}$ (where $s_i \in S$ and $\epsilon_i \in \{-1, 1\}$) by:

$$ \Phi(w) = \varphi(s_1)^{\epsilon_1} \varphi(s_2)^{\epsilon_2} \cdots \varphi(s_n)^{\epsilon_n} $$

We must verify: (1) $\Phi$ is well-defined (independent of reduction), (2) $\Phi$ is a group homomorphism, and (3) $\Phi$ is unique. Well-definedness follows because any two reduced forms of the same element differ by insertions/deletions of $s s^{-1}$ pairs, which map to the identity in $G$. Homomorphism property: $\Phi(w_1 w_2)$ involves concatenating words, which corresponds to multiplication in $G$. Uniqueness: since $S$ generates $F(S)$, any homomorphism is determined by its values on $S$, and $\Phi \circ \iota = \varphi$ fixes these values.
